THE DIGITASLBi UK NEWFRONT

The DigitasLBi UK NewFront is a collaborative thought-leadership programme aimed at stimulating creativity within the burgeoning branded content marketplace.

Founded in the US in 2008, the DigitasLBi NewFront initiative has gone from strength to strength, helping companies of all shapes and sizes harness the very latest branded content opportunities.

DigitasLBi first brought the concept to the UK in 2014 as part of the IAB Digital UpFronts, a week-long series of events aimed at encouraging longer term thinking and planning for digital.

This year the DigitasLBi UK NewFront will take place ahead of the IAB Digital UpFronts, with a focus on innovation in content providing the perfect runway into the IAB’s week of programming.

Bringing together leading brands, creative thinkers, start-ups and media owners the DigitasLBi UK NewFront 2015 will help marketers get to grips with brave and experimental new approaches to content creation.

You’ll be challenged and inspired by speakers including Professor Hugh Herr PhD, head of the biomechatronics research group at MIT Media Lab; comedian Richard Ayoade; NASA astronaut Reid Wiseman; DJ, TV presenter and founder of The Pool, Lauren Laverne; Mumsnet CEO Justine Roberts; and Sabotage Times founder James Brown.

Rob Orchard

Co-founder and editorial director of the Slow Journalism Company

Rob is the publisher of Delayed Gratification magazine, which revisits the events of the preceding quarter after the dust has settled and makes a virtue of being "Last to Breaking News". The publication is a labour of love that's designed to offer an antidote to PR-driven stories, knee-jerk reactions and churnalism. Prior to starting the Slow Journalism Company, Rob launched and ran magazines for Virgin Atlantic, created the Middle East's biggest travel magazine and worked as the editor of Time Out Dubai, Time Out Croatia and Time Out Paris.

Hugh Herr

Associate Professor, MIT Media Lab

Hugh Herr, who heads the Biomechatronics research group at the MIT Media Lab, is creating bionic limbs that emulate the function of natural limbs. In 2011, TIME magazine described Herr as the “Leader of the Bionic Age” because of his revolu¬tionary work in the emerging field of biomechatronics – technology that marries human physiology with electro¬mechanics.

A double amputee himself, he is responsible for breakthrough advances in bionic limbs that provide greater mobility and new hope to those with physical disabilities. He has also designed his own bionic legs, and the world’s first bionic foot and calf system called the BiOM.

Herr is the author and co-author of over 80 peer-reviewed manuscripts and patents, chronicling the science and tech¬nology behind his many innovations. The computer-controlled knee was named one of TIME magazine’s Top Ten Inventions in 2004; the robotic ankle-foot prosthesis was named in the same list in 2007. Also in 2007, Herr was presented with the 13th annual Heinz Award for Technology, the Economy and Employment.

FINDING PURPOSE IN TRANSFORMATION

Transformation is a word that’s used a lot today in marketing circles. This is in part due to the fact that it’s become an acceptable hyperbole for what most agencies now claim to do for their clients, but mainly it’s due to the sweeping changes technology is reaping on our society.

Thanks to the incalculable power of tech our ability to transform the world around us – whether through art, science, economics or engineering - has never been greater. Technology is changing the cars we drive, the products we buy, the way we manage our health and thanks to wearables and bionics even what it constitutes to be human. We are in the midst of a global technological revolution. It’s a cliché but it’s true.

It’s against this backdrop that our own industry is undergoing a fundamental shift. A shift that is seeing technology bring brands and consumers much closer together – disintermediating agencies and lessening the need for the carefully crafted marketing messages that brands have relied on so for so long.

You only have to look at how quickly traditional broadcast advertising has gone from being the center of the marketing universe to just another tab on the media plan. (I believe it was somewhere between the creative director’s third and fourth martini that the ad model went and fundamentally changed). There will of course always be a place for beautifully constructed stories that make products relevant and appealing, but the way we tell these stories, the rules of engagement between brands and consumers, has altered forever.

Thankfully, we’re fast moving away from outdated modes of demand generation that are no longer relevant in a world where we choose which brands we like and which ones we don’t in an instant, with a swipe or a click. Instead, forward-thinking marketers are now altogether smarter about how they approach their customer relationships, providing products and services that add value to people’s lives – things that entertain, educate, inform or engage.

However, in a world where technological transformations are giving life to artificial intelligence, allowing the blind to see and readying manned rockets to mars, brands need to up their game. It’s one thing to be useful but having purpose - a purpose over and above simply selling products, a purpose that somehow makes the world a better place - is where the future really lies for brands. As we move further into a world where entire industries, workforces and economic interdependences are disrupted by technology the way brands do business is as important as the business they do. Being socially aware, doing good whilst making money and fostering a more altruistic approach to marketing is the best way for brands to connect with their customers.

The role for agencies in this world of rapid change becomes much more about helping brands to discover their purpose and identifying what role they can play in people’s lives. Marketers can then tell their stories in ways that are genuine and meaningful as opposed to cleverly crafted artifices that portray their brands to the world in exactly the way they want them to be seen. This is the only real way for brands to resonate with people in the transformation age: to shift from creating utility to providing purpose.

This year the DigitasLBi UK NewFront will explore tales of transformation in business, branding, culture, creativity and life to help marketers get to grips with brave and purposeful new approaches to content creation.
